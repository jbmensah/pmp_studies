2. PM Leadership Responsibilities üëë

The project manager's role requires formal documentation and continuous planning.

  • Documentation & Formal Processes: Resource management activities are formal and require documentation. The processes of resource management are repeated and updated throughout the project.
  • Roles & Responsibilities: Must be clear.
  • Team Planning: Projects are planned by the team and coordinated by the PM. PM formally plans team building activities in advance‚Äîthese are a required part of project management.
  • Recognition: Creating a recognition and reward system is an important and required resource management function.
  • Competencies: PM is responsible for improving team members' competencies.
  • Resources: PM must continually confirm resource availability and control physical resources (not just procurement).

Agile Leadership Focus üßò‚Äç‚ôÇÔ∏è

On agile projects, the PM (or Scrum Master/Agile Coach) is a Servant Leader:

  • Focus on making sure processes are understood and followed.
  • Provide team training where needed.
  • Remove impediments to project progress.
  • Interface with stakeholders like the project sponsor.

3. Plan Resource Management üìã

This process defines roles, responsibilities, and authority, documenting them in the Resource Management Plan.

Key Planning Outcomes (The "Build" Concept) üõ†Ô∏è

Planning answers these questions, covering the 'Build Performance' tasks:

  1. How are we going to do all this as a team?
  2. Who needs to be on the team to accomplish it?
  3. What do I need to do to be a good servant leader on this project?

Essential Artifacts (Inputs) üìù

Factors documented early that inform resource planning:

  • Scope Baseline (helps determine resources needed for deliverables).
  • Stakeholder Register (lists individuals/groups, including their power/interest analysis).
  • Quality Management Plan (defines required quality/grade of physical resources).
  • Organizational Process Assets (e.g., resource management plan templates, policies, lessons learned).
  • Enterprise Environmental Factors (e.g., company culture, team collocation status, availability of contract help).

Methods for Defining Roles üìä

Know what each visualization tool is used for to answer situational questions.

  • Responsibility Assignment Matrix (RAM): Chart cross-referencing team members with activities/work packages (doesn't show time).
  • RACI Chart: A type of RAM that defines roles more clearly.
    • R: Responsible (Multiple resources possible).
    • A: Accountable (Only ONE person held accountable).
    • C: Consult (Multiple resources possible).
    • I: Inform (Multiple resources possible).
  • Organizational Breakdown Structure (OBS): Used to assign responsibilities to organizational divisions (e.g., Marketing, IT).
  • Resource Breakdown Structure (RBS): Hierarchical structure breaking down work by resource type (People, Materials, Equipment, etc.). Referenced from the WBS.

Artifacts of Planning (Outputs) üìÑ

  • Resource Management Plan: Includes a Human Resources/Team Management Plan (roles, acquisition process, training, safety) and a Recognition Plan.
  • Recognition Plan: Details when/how resources will be recognized and what achievements will be rewarded. Personal recognition (like saying "thank you") is highly effective.
  • Team Charter (Ground Rules): A working agreement developed by team members. Defines approach for communication, decision-making, and conflict resolution.
    • Ground rules are especially important for virtual teams.
    • Examples: conflict resolution process, notification of difficulty, meeting rules, status update methods.

4. Estimate Resource Requirements üóìÔ∏èüí∞

In predictive environments, the PM estimates the type and quantity of all resources needed at the work package and activity level.

Key Inputs/Artifacts üóÑÔ∏è

Planning is iterative; refining scope, schedule, and cost happens concurrently.

  • Scope Baseline & Activity List (contains WBS work package estimates).
  • Activity Attributes (specific info like type/quantity of resources expected).
  • Cost Estimates (provides constraints since resource costs must fit within the cost baseline).
  • Resource Calendars (shows organizational work hours, holidays, and resource availability).

Methods & Techniques üìà

  • Resource Histograms: Bar chart visualizing resource requirements vs. availability per time period, showing spikes in need.
  • Resource Leveling: Technique used to change the project to minimize the peaks and valleys of resource usage.
  • Estimating techniques: Analogous, Bottom-up, Parametric, and Expert Judgment.
  • Analyze alternative equipment or methods.

5. Acquire Resources ü§ù (Executing)

This executing process involves securing people (onboarding) and releasing them throughout the project as their work finishes (predictive environment).

Agile vs. Predictive Acquisition üí°

Predictive Environment Agile Environment
Resources enter and leave over time. Teams are stable, relatively small (approx. 8‚Äì12 people).
Focus on securing needed resources via internal negotiation or procurement. Teams are dedicated, have all necessary members (PM, PO, developers, testers), and projects are brought to the team.

Team Configurations üë•

Be aware how team configuration impacts the PM's work (e.g., negotiation with part-time teams, communication complexity with partnerships/virtual teams).

  • Dedicated Teams: Members work full-time exclusively on the project. Common in projectized organizations; important for rapidly changing agile projects.
  • Part-Time Teams: Members split time between the project and other work. Common in functional/matrix organizations.
  • Virtual Teams: Geographic distance necessitates use. PM can negotiate for the best resources regardless of location. Agile organizations generally try to avoid virtual teams, preferring face-to-face.
  • Preassigned Team Members: Resources listed in the Project Charter before the project starts.

Negotiating Internal Resources üó£Ô∏è

The PM negotiates with resource managers:

  • Know the project's needs and priority within the organization.
  • Express how the resource manager will benefit.
  • Be open to creative ways to help the resource manager meet their own needs.
  • DO NOT ask for the best resources if the project does not need them.
üö® Halo Effect: Avoid rating team members high or low on all criteria based on performance in just one specific criterion (e.g., assuming a great programmer will be a great team leader).

6. Develop Team üìà (Executing)

The process of leading, empowering, and motivating the team to achieve high performance levels. Requires all People Domain skills.

Key Development Methods üõ†Ô∏è

  • Collocation: Having the entire team in one place (office/room). Improves communication, decreases conflict, and improves project identity. Adaptive approaches encourage this.
  • Virtual Teams: Must rely on technology (video conferencing, interactive whiteboards, presence-based apps) to simulate collocation benefits and improve communication.
  • Team Building Activities: Required, concerted, and continued effort throughout the project. Start early.
    • Examples: Collaborative problem solving, creating the WBS as a group, retrospectives, milestone parties.
  • Training: Provides needed competencies.
  • Recognition and Rewards: Must be systematic and planned.
  • Individual & Team Assessments: Used to evaluate effectiveness, skill improvement, conflict handling, and identify needed support.

PM‚Äôs Role in Culture Development üíñ

The PM develops a positive team culture by leading by example:

  • Allowing transparency, integrity, and respect.
  • Fostering positive interactions.
  • Creating a safe and open environment for voicing opinions/issues.
  • Using soft skills: mentoring, negotiation, influencing, and active listening.

Team Performance Appraisals vs. Rewards: Project performance appraisals are part of the Manage Team process, while the resulting rewards and additional training are given as part of the Develop Team process.

7. Manage Team ‚úÖ (Executing)

Involves day-to-day management, tracking performance, and resolving issues. It utilizes information from performance assessments to identify successes and conflicts.

Sustaining High Performance (The "Support" Concept) üõ°Ô∏è

  • Observe what is happening (beyond reports).
  • Track and evaluate team performance.
  • Mentor and provide leadership.
  • Facilitate conflict resolution (for conflicts team members cannot resolve themselves).

Tools for Tracking Performance üìä

  • Earned Value Measurement (EVM): Reflects project progression relative to the plan.
  • Burndown Charts (Agile): Track the amount of work to be done; line moves downward as work is completed.
  • Burnup Charts (Agile): Track the amount of work that has been completed; line moves upward. Key Advantage: They can show changes in scope, making the impact visible.

The Issue Log üìã

Used to record problems and their resolutions.

  • Tracks resolution of conflicts and team issues.
  • It facilitates assessment of causes, impact on constraints (scope, schedule, cost, risk), and recommendations for corrective actions.
  • It is frequently an input and an output of the same processes because it is continuously updated.

Outputs of Manage Team üì¶

Includes change requests (if resource needs, cost, or schedule change), updates to the issue log and lessons learned. Plans for releasing team members are documented in the Resource Management Plan.

8. üö® Role Responsibility Cheat Sheet (Situational Questions)

For exam purposes, assume project management is done correctly unless the question indicates otherwise. These answers identify the key role responsible for resolution/decision-making.

Situation / Problem Responsible Role Reasoning / Context
Two project team members are having a disagreement. Team Members They should attempt to solve it themselves first. PM assists if unresolved.
A change to the overall project deliverable is needed. Sponsor This is a change to the Project Charter, which only the sponsor can approve.
A functional manager is trying to pull a team member off the project. Team Members Team members should be empowered with enough information (schedule, risks) to manage their own workloads. If the manager has pulled the resource, the PM steps in.
The Project Manager does not have the authority to get things done. Sponsor The sponsor provides the PM's authority via the Project Charter.
There are not enough resources to complete the project. Sponsor and Functional Manager They control resources (funding and personnel).
An activity needs more time and WILL cause the project to be delayed. Sponsor The project completion date is likely in the Charter; delaying the project is a Charter change requiring sponsor approval (assuming no reserve is available).
A team member is not performing. Project Manager and Functional Manager They share responsibility for directing resources.
The project is behind schedule. Project Manager PM controls the overall project schedule.
The project is running out of funds. Sponsor The sponsor provides funding.
Team member determines a different method should be used to complete an activity. Team Member Team members control their activities as long as they meet time, cost, quality, and scope objectives. They must keep the PM informed.
The team is in conflict over priorities between activities. Project Manager The PM must settle such conflicts and ensure the network diagram/critical path are established.
