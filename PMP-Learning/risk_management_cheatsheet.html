1. Risk Management ‚Äî Key Concepts

üí° Big Picture

Risk Management is about dealing with uncertainty ‚Äî things that may or may not happen but could affect project objectives positively (opportunities) or negatively (threats).

Why Risk Matters
  • Every project operates in a VUCA world: volatile, uncertain, complex, ambiguous.
  • Ignoring risk = being surprised later, often at higher cost and impact.
  • Proactive risk work protects scope, schedule, cost, quality.
Core Concepts
  • Risk Categories & RBS (Risk Breakdown Structure).
  • Risk Register for documenting and tracking risks.
  • Qualitative tools: SWOT, probability & impact matrix.
  • Quantitative tools: decision trees, Monte Carlo, sensitivity.
Responses & Reserves
  • Different strategies for threats vs opportunities.
  • Contingency & management reserves to fund responses.
  • Reserve analysis & burndown charts to see if reserves are adequate.
üß≠ Key takeaway: Risk management is not just a register; it is a continuous cycle of identify ‚Üí analyse ‚Üí plan responses ‚Üí monitor.

2. Risk Categories & Risk Breakdown Structure (RBS)

üìä Brainstorming Frameworks

Risk categories give structure when you are searching for risks. They are usually defined in the Risk Management Plan and often visualised as a Risk Breakdown Structure (RBS).

Common Category Frameworks
  • PESTEL: Political, Economic, Social, Technological, Environmental, Legal.
  • TCP / TECO: Technical, Commercial, Operational, Environmental / Political variants.
  • VUCA: Volatility, Uncertainty, Complexity, Ambiguity (good for overall project risk).
  • Organisation may have its own standard risk taxonomy ‚Äî use it if available.
Risk Breakdown Structure (RBS)
  • Hierarchical chart of sources of risk.
  • Example: Technical ‚Üí Requirements, Technology, Interfaces, Infrastructure.
  • Other branches: Management, Commercial, External, Stakeholder, etc.
  • Used as a brainstorming checklist to ensure you do not miss major sources of risk.
üß≠ Exam hint: When the question asks about ‚Äúgrouping risks‚Äù or ‚Äúidentifying sources of overall project risk‚Äù, think of risk categories, RBS, or frameworks like PESTEL / VUCA.

3. Risk Register

üìã Core Artifact

The Risk Register is the central document where you record details of each identified project risk. It can be simple or very detailed depending on the project.

Minimum Content
  • Unique ID (e.g., R-001, R-002).
  • Risk owner ‚Äî person accountable for monitoring & response.
  • Risk response(s) ‚Äî how we plan to handle the risk.
Optional but Useful Fields
  • Risk title & description.
  • Category, triggers, and related WBS activities.
  • Probability, impact, and overall risk score.
  • Current status (open, closed, escalated, etc.).
üß≠ Exam hint: The Risk Register is a key output from Identify Risks, and is updated in later risk processes (analysis, planning responses, monitoring).

4. SWOT Analysis

üìä Qualitative Brainstorming

SWOT analysis is used to recognise risks by looking at the project‚Äôs Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, and Threats.

Internal View: S & W
  • Strengths: What are we good at? (Capabilities, assets, expertise.)
  • Weaknesses: Where are we vulnerable? (Gaps in skills, resources, processes.)
  • Weaknesses often point to threats if not managed.
External View: O & T
  • Opportunities: Positive risks we can exploit or enhance.
  • Threats: Negative risks that could harm objectives.
  • Turn strengths into opportunities; manage weaknesses so they do not turn into threats.
üí° Tip: SWOT is quick, intuitive, and works well in workshops for generating an initial risk list.

5. Probability & Impact Matrix

üìä Prioritising Risks

The Probability & Impact Matrix is a grid used in qualitative analysis to compare and prioritise risks.

How It Works
  • For each risk, estimate:
    • Probability ‚Äì how likely it is to occur.
    • Impact ‚Äì how big the consequence is if it happens.
  • Use scales like Very Low ‚Üí Very High or numeric 1‚Äì5.
  • You can compute a risk score = Probability √ó Impact (e.g., 4 √ó 3 = 12).
Why It‚Äôs Useful
  • Risks with high P and high I get the most attention.
  • Visual matrix helps stakeholders quickly see the ‚Äúred zone‚Äù.
  • Supports decisions on which risks to plan detailed responses for.
üß≠ Exam hint: If a question talks about ‚Äúrating risks based on likelihood and impact‚Äù or ‚Äúprioritising the risk list‚Äù, they are usually hinting at the probability‚Äìimpact matrix.

6. Decision Trees

üßÆ Quantitative Decisions

Decision trees support quantitative risk analysis for choosing between several options where each option has different costs, payoffs, and probabilities.

Structure
  • Diagram uses:
    • Decision nodes (squares) ‚Äì where you choose among alternatives.
    • Chance nodes (circles) ‚Äì where different outcomes occur with probabilities.
  • Each branch has costs or benefits and probabilities attached.
  • End of each path gives an outcome value (e.g., profit or cost).
How It‚Äôs Used
  • Calculate the Expected Monetary Value (EMV) for each path.
  • EMV = Œ£ (probability √ó outcome) across all possible outcomes.
  • Pick the option with the best EMV (usually highest profit or lowest cost).
  • Shows both the size of payoff and the riskiness of each decision.
üß≠ Exam hint: When you see ‚Äúchoose between two alternatives with different probabilities and payoffs‚Äù, think decision tree + EMV.

7. Quantitative Risk Tools

üßÆ Advanced Analysis

Quantitative risk analysis is used mainly on larger or high-risk projects to understand the numerical impact of risk on schedule and cost.

Monte Carlo Simulation
  • Runs thousands of simulations of the project with different random inputs.
  • Produces a probability distribution of possible end dates or budgets.
  • Helps answer questions like: ‚ÄúWhat is the chance we finish before 30 Sept?‚Äù
  • Results are often shown as histograms and cumulative probability curves.
Sensitivity Analysis & Tornado Charts
  • Looks at which individual risks have the greatest effect on objectives.
  • Tornado chart:
    • Bars show impact range of each risk (positive or negative).
    • Sorted from largest to smallest impact ‚Üí shape looks like a tornado.
  • Helps focus responses on the few risks that drive most of the uncertainty.

8. Responses for Threats & Opportunities

üí° Strategy Toolkit

We use different strategies for threats (negative risks) and opportunities (positive risks). Some strategies are shared (e.g., escalate, accept), others are specific.

Strategies for Opportunities (Positive Risks)
  • Escalate: Hand over to higher level (e.g., programme portfolio) if outside the project‚Äôs scope or authority.
  • Exploit: Make sure the opportunity happens ‚Äî assign top resources, prioritise, fund it.
  • Share: Transfer ownership to a third party who can help realise it (e.g., joint venture).
  • Enhance: Increase the probability or impact of the opportunity.
  • Accept: Take no proactive action, but acknowledge and monitor it.
Strategies for Threats (Negative Risks)
  • Escalate: Raise to senior management if outside project control.
  • Avoid: Change the plan to remove the threat (e.g., change scope, supplier, approach).
  • Transfer: Shift the impact to a third party (e.g., insurance, warranty, fixed-price contract).
  • Mitigate: Reduce probability or impact (e.g., extra testing, prototyping, simplifying design).
  • Accept: Do nothing beyond monitoring (often for low-priority risks or when mitigation is not cost-effective).
üß≠ Exam hint: Watch for wording: Exploit / Enhance / Share are for opportunities; Avoid / Transfer / Mitigate are for threats. Escalate and Accept can apply to both.

9. Reserve Analysis & Burndown

üìä Funding Risk Responses

Reserve analysis checks whether we have enough contingency reserve left to cover the remaining risks in the project.

Contingency & Management Reserves
  • Contingency reserve:
    • Part of the cost baseline.
    • Funds identified risks (from the risk register).
  • Management reserve:
    • Not part of the cost baseline, but part of total budget.
    • Used for unknown / unforeseen work within scope.
Reserve Burndown
  • Compare remaining risks vs remaining contingency.
  • Visualise using a burndown chart:
    • Line for total risk exposure over time.
    • Line for remaining contingency reserve.
  • If risk line stays high but reserve line drops too fast ‚Üí underfunded.
  • Helps justify adjusting reserves or changing risk responses.
üí° Tip: Reserve analysis is about ‚ÄúDo we still have enough money to cover the risks we have left?‚Äù rather than just tracking how much contingency we‚Äôve spent.

üî• Exam-Focused Summary ‚Äî Risk Management

Concept Flow
  • Use risk categories / RBS / SWOT to identify a wide set of risks.
  • Record them in the Risk Register with owners and potential responses.
  • Apply Probability‚ÄìImpact matrix to prioritise which risks matter most.
  • For bigger projects, use quantitative tools (decision trees, Monte Carlo, sensitivity).
  • Choose appropriate response strategies and fund them via reserves.
  • Monitor via reserve analysis & burndown to ensure you stay covered.
Mindset & Exam Signals
  • Risk is about uncertainty, not just bad events.
  • Opportunities are just as important as threats ‚Äî know their distinct responses.
  • When you see ‚Äúchoose between alternatives with cost & probabilities‚Äù ‚Üí decision tree, EMV.
  • When you see ‚Äúrate risks based on likelihood & impact‚Äù ‚Üí probability‚Äìimpact matrix.
  • When you see ‚Äúhave we got enough reserve left?‚Äù ‚Üí reserve analysis / burndown chart.
üéì One-liner: Risk Management is about seeing uncertainty early, choosing smart responses, and making sure you have the reserves to pay for them as the project progresses.
