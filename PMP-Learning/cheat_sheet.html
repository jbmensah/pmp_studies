‚öñÔ∏è Solution Options & Recommendations
Prioritization & Decision

Once the problem and context are understood, the business case explores solution options and recommends what to do.

‚úÖ Required Must-have

Essential items needed to address the core problem.

  • Without these, the project cannot meet its objective.
  • Usually tied directly to compliance, core service, or critical value.

‚≠ê Desired Nice-to-have

Important but not mandatory items.

  • Improve user experience, performance, or efficiency.
  • Can be included if budget/time allow.

‚ú® Optional Bonus

Small extra benefits or enhancements.

  • Lowest priority, easiest to drop if constrained.
  • Useful for incremental improvements later.
üéØ
Exam angle: Required / Desired / Optional is an easy prioritization model you may see in scenario questions where you must decide what to include first under constraints.
üìå Typical Options Considered
  • Do nothing (business as usual):
    • Document impact (e.g., lost market share, penalties).
  • Do the minimum:
    • Implement only required items to reduce risk/cost.
  • Do more than minimum:
    • Include desired/optional items for extra benefit.
‚úÖ Final Recommendation
  • Summarizes which option is preferred and why.
  • Shows expected financial impact:
    • Gains (revenue, savings, share)
    • Losses/risks if not done
  • Sponsor/funder uses this to approve or reject the project.
üöÄ Business Case in Agile Environments
Agile & Hybrid View
üß© Feature-Based Value Delivery
Product Owner Focus

In agile, the business case still matters, but it is often applied at the feature or release level, not just to a one-time big project.

  • Backlog items/features are ordered by customer value.
  • Each feature can have a mini business case:
    • Expected benefit
    • Estimated effort/cost
  • High-value, low-cost items are implemented first.
  • Value is delivered incrementally, early and often.
üîÅ Continuous Re-Justification
  • As the product evolves, the team and PO revisit:
    • Is this still worth doing?
    • Should we pivot or stop?
  • The business case thinking is continuous, not one-off.
  • Helps avoid sunk-cost fallacy in long-running initiatives.
üìå Agile ‚â† ‚ÄúNo Business Case‚Äù üéØ Always maximize value per sprint/release üìà Prioritization = Cost vs Benefit vs Risk
üß†
If a scenario shows an agile project and asks how to ensure the team delivers the highest value first, the best answer often involves the product owner using business case logic to prioritize the backlog by value.
