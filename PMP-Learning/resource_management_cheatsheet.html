2. Responsibility Assignment Matrix (RAM) and RACI

üìä Who Does What

The Responsibility Assignment Matrix (RAM), commonly in the form of a RACI chart, links work packages or activities to project team members. It answers: ‚ÄúWhat are we doing, and who is assigned to it?‚Äù

RACI Components
  • Responsible (R): does the work or creates the deliverable.
  • Accountable (A): owns the outcome, signs off, ‚Äúthe buck stops here‚Äù.
  • Consulted (C): provides expert input; two-way communication.
  • Informed (I): kept up to date; one-way communication.
How RAM / RACI is Used
  • List activities or deliverables along one axis (e.g. columns).
  • List roles or people along the other axis (e.g. rows).
  • Mark R, A, C, I per cell to show each person‚Äôs involvement.
  • Can be high-level (features, work packages) or detailed (team-level tasks).
  • Reduces confusion and overlap by clarifying who owns what.
üß≠ Exam hint: If the question mentions ‚Äúclarify roles‚Äù, ‚Äúremove confusion about responsibilities‚Äù, or ‚Äúwho is accountable‚Äù, think RACI / RAM.

3. Team Charter

üìú Ways of Working

The Team Charter is a document that captures the team‚Äôs values, working agreements, and operating guidelines ‚Äî how the team chooses to work together.

Typical Contents
  • Team Vision / Mission ‚Äî what we aim to achieve.
  • Definition of Done (DoD) ‚Äî what ‚Äúdone‚Äù looks like.
  • Roles & Responsibilities ‚Äî may link to a RACI.
  • Stakeholders ‚Äî SMEs, approvers, recipients of output.
  • Team values ‚Äî how we want to behave and collaborate.
Decision & Conflict Handling
  • How decisions are made (consensus, majority, product owner, sponsor, etc.).
  • Who can approve or sign off on what.
  • Conflict resolution steps if the team cannot agree.
  • Definition of key ceremonies:
    • Stand-ups ‚Äî day & time, purpose.
    • Retrospectives, sprint planning, reviews ‚Äî cadence and format.

The charter works best when co-created by the team and reviewed regularly (e.g. in retrospectives).

üß≠ Key takeaway: The team charter is a living agreement, not a one-off document. It sets expectations before conflicts and misunderstandings arise.

4. Tuckman‚Äôs Ladder

ü§ù Team Development

Tuckman‚Äôs Ladder describes the stages a team moves through from first coming together to finishing the project. Knowing these stages helps you recognise and guide team dynamics.

Core Stages
  • Forming: team meets; roles are unclear; people work more independently.
  • Storming: work styles clash; conflict arises; power struggles and disagreements.
  • Norming: roles become clear; conflicts are resolved; cooperation increases.
  • Performing: team is high-functioning; norms are established; focus on goals.
  • Adjourning: project ends; team disbands; members move on to new work.
How It Helps the PM
  • Expect conflict and confusion in early stages (storming).
  • Use facilitation and conflict management to move toward norming.
  • Support the team with clear goals and empowerment to reach performing.
  • Recognise adjourning as a real phase ‚Äî allow wrap-up, recognition, lessons learned.
üß≠ Exam hint: Situational questions describing ‚Äúnew team, confusion, conflict‚Äù are often pointing to the Forming / Storming stages and need supportive leadership.

5. Meeting Management

üìÖ Productive Sessions

Meetings are a key tool for problem solving, decision making, and consensus building. Meeting management is about making sure meetings are effective and efficient.

Common Problems
  • No clear purpose or agenda.
  • Wrong people in the room (missing decision makers or SMEs).
  • Meetings drag on, off-topic discussions, no decisions.
  • No record of actions; no follow-up.
Good Meeting Practices
  • Prepare and share an agenda with clear objectives and outcomes.
  • Start and finish on time ‚Äî respect participants‚Äô time.
  • Invite the right participants (decision makers, SMEs, key stakeholders).
  • Use facilitation skills to keep discussion on topic.
  • Ensure everyone has a voice; avoid domination by the loudest or most senior.
  • Record actions, owners, and due dates and share them.
  • Follow up after the meeting to confirm progress on agreed actions.
üß≠ Key takeaway: A ‚Äúgood meeting‚Äù ends with clear decisions, agreed actions, and owners, not just discussion.

6. Conflict Management

ü§ù Handling Tension

Conflict is inevitable in projects ‚Äî new teams, competing priorities, scarce resources, and different working styles all contribute. The project manager‚Äôs job is to manage conflict constructively.

General Principles
  • Address conflict early and usually in private.
  • Use direct, clear communication ‚Äî avoid vague language.
  • Take a collaborative approach where possible.
  • Be aware of organisational procedures if escalation or discipline is needed.
  • Consider:
    • Importance / intensity of the conflict.
    • Time pressure to resolve.
    • Relative power of the parties.
    • Need to preserve long-term relationships.
Five Conflict Resolution Techniques
  • Withdrawing / Avoiding: retreating or postponing; suitable for low-priority issues or cooling-off.
  • Smoothing / Accommodating: emphasise agreement, yield to maintain harmony; lose‚Äìwin.
  • Compromising / Reconciling: each side gives something up; often lose‚Äìlose but workable.
  • Forcing / Directing: use authority or power to decide; win‚Äìlose, used in emergencies.
  • Collaborating / Problem Solving: integrate multiple viewpoints, jointly find solution; win‚Äìwin, usually best long-term choice.
üß≠ Exam hint: When the question asks for the best, long-term approach to conflict, the preferred answer is usually Collaborating / Problem Solving.
