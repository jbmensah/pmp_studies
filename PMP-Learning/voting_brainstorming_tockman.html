What is Brainstorming?

Brainstorming is a group creativity technique used to generate many ideas quickly before evaluating them. It is a core tool in:

  • Collect Requirements ‚Äì product & project requirements
  • Identify Risks ‚Äì risk sources, threats, and opportunities
  • Problem solving & solution design across the project
Quantity first No criticism during idea flow Encourages participation Often followed by voting
üìò Exam lens

If the question shows a team stuck, silent, or limited to one person‚Äôs ideas, PMI expects the PM to use a collaborative technique like brainstorming before deciding alone.

Key Elements (Study Table)

Concept Description & PMP Application
Purpose Elicit and analyze requirements, generate ideas, identify risks, or find solutions ‚Äì especially during business analysis before scope is fully defined.
Techniques Use people with different perspectives (users, SMEs, devs). A variation is Brain writing: participants think and write ideas individually, then share with the group.
Goal Maximize the number of ideas; evaluation comes later via prioritization or voting.
Post-session Use Nominal Group Technique or other voting to rank the most useful ideas, then convert them into requirements, backlog items, or risk responses.
Whenever you see “PM wants to gather ideas from stakeholders” ‚Üí think Brainstorming. Slide 3

Analogy: The Flash Flood of Ideas

üåßÔ∏è Phase 1 ‚Äì Flood (Idea Generation)

Think of brainstorming as a flash flood rushing through a dry riverbed. The goal is to let as much water (ideas) flow as possible.

  • No dams ‚Äì no judgment, no debate, no early filtering.
  • All ideas are welcome, even wild or unrealistic ones.
  • Silence the inner critic ‚Äì defer analysis.
üßπ Phase 2 ‚Äì Sorting Debris (Analysis)

After the flood, you sort through what was carried downstream: categorize, clarify, and select the most valuable ideas.

  • Cluster ideas (affinity diagrams, mind maps).
  • Use Nominal Group Technique or voting to rank them.
  • Turn top ideas into requirements, actions, or risks.

PM Do / Don‚Äôt Checklist

‚úÖ
Do
  • State a clear problem/question before the session.
  • Invite diverse stakeholders, not only technical team.
  • Encourage quiet members (brain writing if needed).
  • Capture every idea visibly (board, sticky notes, tool).
‚õî
Don‚Äôt
  • Allow criticism or evaluation during idea generation.
  • Let one loud stakeholder dominate the session.
  • Stop after generating ideas ‚Äì always follow with prioritization.

Exam Tip
If a scenario shows a PM jumping straight to a solution without consulting stakeholders, a better answer is often: “Facilitate a brainstorming session with key stakeholders.”

Why Voting Matters

Voting is a collective decision-making technique used to turn many ideas or options into a ranked, agreed, and prioritized list.

It helps the team make decisions quickly while using the diverse knowledge of the group.

üìä PMP Use Cases
  • Prioritizing requirements after brainstorming.
  • Choosing risks to analyze in detail.
  • Deciding between solution alternatives.
  • Improving commitment to estimates or cost figures.
Engages the team Supports consensus building Often combined with multicriteria analysis

Types of Voting (Predictive / Hybrid)

Type Definition & When to Use
Unanimity Everyone agrees on a single course of action. Best for high-stakes decisions (e.g., team charter, core security standards) where buy-in is critical.
Majority More than 50% of the group agrees. Efficient when you want a fast decision and the group is not too fragmented.
Plurality The largest block decides, even if not a majority. Used when there are many options and no clear majority emerges.

The PM may also use autocratic decision making (one person decides) or multicriteria decision analysis (using weighted criteria) depending on context.

Exam clue: many options + no majority ‚Üí often “plurality” is the tool. Slide 5

Voting in Agile / Adaptive Environments

üÉè Planning Poker¬Æ

Team members secretly select estimate cards (e.g., story points) and reveal them at the same time. Differences are discussed and the team votes again until consensus is reached.

üìå MoSCoW Analysis
  • M ‚Äì Must have
  • S ‚Äì Should have
  • C ‚Äì Could have
  • W ‚Äì Would like to have (later)

This is a structured way of ‚Äúvoting‚Äù on importance and urgency of items in a product backlog or high-level requirements list.

Analogy: Choosing Restaurant Specials üçΩÔ∏è

Imagine a restaurant team deciding which dishes should be the special of the day:

  • For low-stakes items (e.g., appetizer of the day), the team might use Majority or Plurality to decide quickly.
  • For high-stakes items (e.g., signature dish or brand-defining menu change), they may require Unanimity to ensure full commitment.
üß© Situational Insight

In exam scenarios, when the team is stuck between several strong options, the PM should facilitate a voting session or use multicriteria decision analysis rather than deciding alone.

Good PMs decide with the team whenever possible, not for the team. Slide 6

The 5 Stages

Tuckman‚Äôs Ladder describes how a team evolves from strangers to a high-performing unit. Understanding where your team is on this ladder helps you choose the right leadership style.

1Ô∏è‚É£
Forming
First meeting, polite behaviour, roles unclear. Members are learning about the project, their responsibilities, and each other.
2Ô∏è‚É£
Storming
Conflicts appear over approaches, priorities, and power. This is normal and expected.
3Ô∏è‚É£
Norming
Team starts to work effectively, trust grows, and ‚Äúteam agreements‚Äù (norms) are created and followed.
4Ô∏è‚É£
Performing
High-performing, self-directed team with strong collaboration and fast problem solving.
5Ô∏è‚É£
Adjourning
Work is completed, the team disbands or moves to new projects. Emotions can be mixed: pride and sometimes sadness.

PM Focus at Each Stage

Stage PM Focus
Forming Directing ‚Äì clarify roles, purpose, and expectations. Establish or reinforce the Team Charter.
Storming Coaching ‚Äì manage conflict, encourage open communication, protect psychological safety.
Norming Supporting ‚Äì reinforce norms, encourage collaboration, let the team handle minor issues.
Performing Delegating/Empowering ‚Äì remove impediments, secure resources, let the team drive execution.
Adjourning Capture lessons learned, recognize contributions, support transitions to new assignments.

Teams can move back and forth between stages (e.g., new member joins ‚Üí temporary regression to Storming).

Hint: “Increasing conflict” usually means the team is in Storming. Slide 7

Sports Team Progression

Imagine a sports team preparing for a championship season ‚Äì this mirrors Tuckman‚Äôs Ladder clearly:

  • Forming (Tryouts): Players meet, learn the playbook, and discover the coach‚Äôs expectations.
  • Storming (Pre-season clashes): Players argue about positions, tactics, and roles.
  • Norming (Chemistry builds): The team agrees on tactics, practice routines, and discipline rules.
  • Performing (Championship mode): Players anticipate each other‚Äôs movements and execute plays smoothly.
  • Adjourning (Season ends): Team breaks up or changes, but lessons and relationships remain.

Situational Leadership Tie-in

üéõÔ∏è PM as Coach

A strong project manager adjusts leadership style as the team climbs Tuckman‚Äôs Ladder:

  • Forming ‚Üí Directing (more guidance, structure).
  • Storming ‚Üí Coaching (conflict resolution, listening).
  • Norming ‚Üí Supporting (encourage ownership).
  • Performing ‚Üí Delegating (trust and empower).

Exam scenarios often describe a team behaviour and expect you to infer: “Which stage is this? What leadership response is appropriate?”

If the PM micro-manages a performing team, that‚Äôs usually not the PMI answer. Slide 8

Typical Flow in a Project Scenario

  1. Team is in Forming: PM clarifies goals and invites stakeholders to contribute ideas.
  2. PM runs a Brainstorming session to gather requirements, risks, or solution options.
  3. Team moves through Storming as they debate ideas. PM uses facilitation and conflict management.
  4. PM organizes ideas and uses Voting (e.g., majority, Planning Poker¬Æ, MoSCoW) to prioritize.
  5. As the team agrees on direction, they enter Norming and later Performing with clear roles and shared ownership.
