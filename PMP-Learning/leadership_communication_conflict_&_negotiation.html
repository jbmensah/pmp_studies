Leadership Core Concepts

Leadership Concept Key Details & PMP Application
PM's Dual Role The PM must manage and lead. Management plans, organizes, and controls work; leadership guides, motivates, and influences people to achieve the vision. The PM balances conflicting stakeholder goals and builds consensus.
Servant Leadership Common in Agile/adaptive environments. The PM focuses on removing impediments, enabling the team, protecting them from distractions, and helping them grow beyond their current roles.
Emotional Intelligence (EI) Ability to perceive, understand, and manage emotions in oneself and others. EI supports trust, fairness, and strong relationships ‚Äì all core to effective leadership.
Leadership Behaviours Setting a motivating vision, seeking resources, influencing without authority, negotiating and resolving conflict. Aligned with the PMP principle: Demonstrate Leadership Behaviours.
Influence > Authority Vision & Direction Empathy & EI Conflict & Negotiation

Think of the project team as an orchestra and the PM as the conductor:

  • The conductor sets the tempo and interpretation (vision and pace).
  • Each musician is a specialist (BA, developer, tester, SME) with their own “instrument”.
  • The conductor ensures harmony and timing but does not play every instrument.
  • Success comes from coordination, trust, and clear cues, not from one person doing everything.

Communication Concepts

Concept Key Details & Situational Use
Communication Flow Flows vertically (upward to management / downward to team) and horizontally (across peers and other departments). Good PMs tailor message content and tone depending on direction.
Adaptive Communication Adaptive/Agile environments need frequent, fast communication (e.g., daily stand-ups, demos). Favour interactive channels (face-to-face, video) that allow rapid feedback.
Communication Gaps The gulf of execution (what user wants vs what the system does) and the gulf of evaluation (can users tell if it worked?) highlight why clear, user-focused communication is crucial.
Active Listening Involves acknowledging, clarifying, and confirming the message. The PM paraphrases back to ensure mutual understanding and reduce misinterpretation.
The 5 Cs Written communication should be: Correct, Concise, Clear, Coherent, and Controlled. Poor grammar or vague messages weaken the PM‚Äôs credibility.

Communication Channels & Complexity

üîó Channels Formula

As you add stakeholders, communication complexity grows quickly. The number of potential one-to-one channels is:

Channels = N (N - 1) / 2    where N = number of stakeholders.

More channels ‚Üí more risk of confusion ‚Üí stronger need for a Communications Management Plan.

üéß Exam Tip

If the scenario shows stakeholders are confused or complaining about not being informed, the best response is usually to review and adjust the communications plan, not simply ‚Äúsend more emails‚Äù.

Conflict in Projects

PMI views conflict as inevitable ‚Äì and potentially healthy when managed constructively.

Conflict Concept Key Details & Situational Insight
Sources of Conflict Common: scarce resources, scheduling priorities, and personal work styles. Personality clashes are actually less common than disagreements over workload and priorities.
Timely Resolution Good PMs address conflict early and privately where possible, focusing on the issue rather than the person.
Healthy Conflict Constructive debate can lead to better decisions and innovation ‚Äì as long as it is managed respectfully and results in clear decisions.

Conflict Resolution Approaches

ü§ù
Collaborating / Problem-solving (Win‚ÄìWin)
Parties openly discuss differences and integrate views. Often the best PMI answer when the issue is important and time allows.
‚öñÔ∏è
Compromising / Reconciling (Partial Win‚ÄìWin / Lose‚ÄìLose)
Each side gives up something. Second-best option when there‚Äôs limited time or when a balanced, middle-ground solution is acceptable.
üö´
Forcing / Directing (Win‚ÄìLose)
Imposing a decision using authority. Typically worst choice, but can be necessary for safety, compliance, or urgent crises.
üïäÔ∏è
Smoothing / Accommodating
Emphasize areas of agreement and downplay disagreement to preserve relationships. Useful short-term, but does not address root cause.
üèÉüèΩ‚Äç‚ôÇÔ∏è
Withdrawing / Avoiding
Step away from the conflict. Suitable only when the issue is minor or more time/data is needed. Long-term avoidance is a red flag.

Analogy: Baking a Cake Together

Imagine two people with different cake recipes:

  • Forcing: One person insists their entire recipe is used; the other‚Äôs input is ignored.
  • Compromising: They mix half of each recipe ‚Äì maybe acceptable, but not ideal.
  • Collaborating: They take the best ingredients and techniques from both recipes to create an even better cake than either original.

In projects, collaboration means combining the strongest ideas from each party into a superior solution.

üìë Internal & External

Negotiation Contexts

Context Key Details & PMP Application
Core Goal Aim for a Win‚ÄìWin outcome where each side is reasonably satisfied. Even when contracts are involved, relationships and long-term collaboration matter.
Internal Negotiation Negotiating with functional managers for staff, budget, or equipment. The PM should understand their constraints and needs (e.g., peak workload) to build a sustainable agreement.
Procurement Negotiation Clarifying rights, obligations, and terms before signing the contract. Goals: fair price, acceptable risk allocation, and a solid working relationship.
Agile Value Agile values customer collaboration over contract negotiation. Contracts still exist, but emphasis is on ongoing dialogue and flexibility to handle changing requirements.

Negotiation Mindset

üß† Before Negotiation
  • Know your objectives and constraints (scope, cost, time, quality).
  • Understand the other party‚Äôs interests, not just their demands.
  • Identify your alternatives if negotiation fails.
üìå On the Exam

If the scenario shows a disagreement with a functional manager, sponsor, or seller, and there is still room to talk, the PM should usually negotiate a mutually acceptable solution before escalating.

Negotiation is about protecting project objectives and relationships. Slide 9

  • You‚Äôre both ‚Äúinvesting‚Äù in a relationship that should deliver long-term value, not just a one-off transaction.

One Scenario, Four Skills

Imagine this project situation:

  • The team is under pressure; two members are arguing about the design. The sponsor is worried about delays and feels out of the loop.

A strong PMI-style response might look like:

  1. Leadership: PM stays calm, acknowledges concerns, and re-focuses everyone on the project goal.
  2. Communication: Schedules an interactive meeting with key stakeholders and updates the communication plan for more frequent sponsor updates.
  3. Conflict Management: Facilitates a collaborative problem-solving session for the design dispute.
  4. Negotiation: Negotiates priorities and timelines with the sponsor or functional managers if capacity is constrained.
