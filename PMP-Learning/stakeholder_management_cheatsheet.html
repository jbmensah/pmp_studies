1. Stakeholder Management ‚Äî Key Concepts

üí° Big Picture

Stakeholders are individuals, groups, or organizations that can affect or be affected by the project. They can make or break your project, so identifying, analyzing, and engaging them is critical.

Why Stakeholders Matter
  • Control or influence funding, resources, approvals.
  • Can support or resist your project.
  • Determine whether the project is considered a success.
Key Activities
  • Identify stakeholders (who are they?).
  • Analyze & categorize them (impact, influence, interest).
  • Plan how to engage them.
  • Monitor and adapt engagement over time.
Core Tools
  • üì¶ Organizational Breakdown Structure (OBS).
  • üìä Stakeholder mapping (impact / influence / salience).
  • üìã Stakeholder Register.
  • üßÆ Stakeholder Engagement Assessment Matrix.
üß≠ Key takeaway: Stakeholder work is not ‚Äúone-off‚Äù at the start ‚Äî it is a continuous process throughout the project.

2. Organizational Breakdown Structure (OBS)

üìä Finding Stakeholders

The Organizational Breakdown Structure (OBS) depicts an organization‚Äôs existing departments, units, and teams. It looks like an org chart and is a powerful tool for discovering stakeholders.

What the OBS Shows
  • Executive managers and leadership.
  • Middle managers / team leaders.
  • Operational teams and staff.
  • Which departments use or are impacted by the project‚Äôs product.
How We Use It
  • Identify stakeholders who:
    • Use the product or process.
    • Provide resources or funding.
    • Offer vital information or expertise.
  • Understand where stakeholders sit in the hierarchy.
  • Highlight early which areas are most impacted by the project.
üß≠ Exam hint: When you see ‚Äúfind who is impacted‚Äù or ‚Äúidentify stakeholders,‚Äù the OBS (or org chart) is a strong early tool to mention.

3. Stakeholder Mapping

üìä Prioritizing Stakeholders

Stakeholder mapping categorizes stakeholders so we can prioritize engagement and communication. We look at dimensions like impact, influence, power, interest, urgency, and legitimacy.

Impact vs Influence Grid

A very practical and commonly used map:

  • Impact: How much the project affects the stakeholder.
  • Influence: How much the stakeholder can affect the project (e.g., approve, delay, cancel).
  • We rank each stakeholder 1‚Äì10 for impact and influence, then plot them.
  • Stakeholders who are high-impact and high-influence are the most critical: they need frequent communication and active engagement.
Other Mapping Approaches
  • Power‚ÄìInterest or Power‚ÄìInfluence grids:
    • High power, high interest ‚Üí manage closely.
    • High power, low interest ‚Üí keep satisfied.
    • Low power, high interest ‚Üí keep informed.
    • Low power, low interest ‚Üí monitor only.
Salience Model (Power, Urgency, Legitimacy)
  • Power: Authority to influence the project.
  • Urgency: Need for immediate attention.
  • Legitimacy: Appropriateness of their involvement.
  • Stakeholders with all three are definitive and must be actively managed.
Directions of Influence
  • Upward: Sponsors, senior management, steering committee.
  • Sideways: Peers of the PM ‚Äì functional managers, middle managers.
  • Downward: Project team and specialist contributors.
  • Outward: Suppliers, end users, regulators, government, public.

Why this matters: Engagement style and communication strategy may differ depending on direction of influence (e.g., how you speak to sponsors vs. technical team vs. regulators).

4. Stakeholder Register

üìã Core Artifact

The Stakeholder Register is the documented list of stakeholders and key information about them. It is a living document that evolves as new stakeholders are identified and others change.

Identification Information
  • Name and organizational position.
  • Role in the project (sponsor, SME, user, developer, etc.).
  • Contact details.
  • Direction of influence (upward, sideways, downward, outward).
  • Internal vs external stakeholder.
Assessment & Classification
  • Major requirements or expectations.
  • Stages where they are most needed (initiation, discovery, testing, etc.).
  • Impact, influence, interest ratings.
  • Engagement level (e.g., from the engagement matrix).
üß≠ Exam hint: Output of Identify Stakeholders often includes the Stakeholder Register plus initial classifications.

5. Stakeholder Engagement Assessment Matrix

üìä Engagement Levels

The Stakeholder Engagement Assessment Matrix compares each stakeholder‚Äôs current vs desired level of engagement, helping you plan how to move them toward the desired state.

Engagement Levels
  • Unaware: Does not know about the project.
  • Resistant: Opposes or obstructs the project.
  • Neutral: No strong opinion; not for or against.
  • Supportive: Supports the project and outcomes.
  • Leading: Actively champions and drives the project.
How It‚Äôs Used
  • For each stakeholder, record:
    • Current engagement level.
    • Desired engagement level.
  • Identify gaps ‚Äî e.g., Current: Resistant ‚Üí Desired: Supportive.
  • Design targeted actions:
    • More frequent communication.
    • Workshops, demos, pilots.
    • Relationship-building and listening sessions.

Example: ‚ÄúBilly‚Äù is currently Resistant, desired is Supportive. Plan: more involvement in decisions, better information, addressing his concerns.

üß≠ Key takeaway: The matrix is not just a table; it is a planning tool to change behaviour and attitudes through deliberate engagement.

6. Interpersonal and Team Skills

ü§ù Soft Skills Toolkit

Stakeholder management is heavily dependent on interpersonal and team skills. Project managers rarely have direct authority over everyone, so they must rely on influence, relationships, and communication.

Core Interaction Skills
  • Conflict management: surface issues and resolve them constructively.
  • Facilitation: guide workshops to decisions and consensus.
  • Meeting management: clear agenda, right people, on time, action items.
  • Active listening: reflect, clarify, confirm understanding.
Leadership & Influence
  • Leadership: communicate vision, build trust, maintain optimism.
  • Critical thinking: challenge assumptions, seek evidence.
  • Influencing: persuade without authority, maintain trust.
  • Motivation: encourage ownership, autonomy, and engagement.
  • Team building: build collaboration and cohesion.
Context & Awareness
  • Networking: build informal support and information channels.
  • Political awareness: understand power structures and gatekeepers.
  • Cultural awareness: adapt style to different cultures and preferences.
  • Emotional intelligence: self-awareness, self-control, empathy, and social skills.
  • Observation & conversation: go to where work happens (e.g., job shadowing).
Communication Style & Negotiation
Communication Styles Assessment
  • Ask stakeholders how they prefer to receive information (email, meetings, dashboards, summaries).
  • Adjust frequency, channel, and level of detail to match their style.
  • Use surveys, direct questions, or observation to learn preferences.
Negotiation
  • Used for resources, scope changes, priorities, timelines.
  • Involves trade-offs and concessions from both sides.
  • Know your BATNA (Best Alternative To a Negotiated Agreement).
  • Goal: reach a mutually beneficial agreement or, if not possible, fall back to BATNA.
